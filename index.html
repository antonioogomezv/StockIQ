Financial Terms

Valuation
P/E Ratio +
Price-to-Earnings ratio. How much investors pay for $1 of company profit. A high P/E means people expect big growth; a low P/E could mean the stock is a bargain — or that the company is struggling.
Under 15 — potentially cheap15–30 — fair valueOver 30 — priced for high growth
Always compare P/E to other companies in the same industry. A P/E of 30 is normal for tech but expensive for banking.
Market Cap +
The total dollar value of all a company's shares. It tells you the "size" of the company. Calculated as: stock price × number of shares.
Large cap +$10B — stable, lower riskMid cap $2B–$10B — balancedSmall cap under $2B — higher risk, higher reward
Large cap stocks like Apple or Microsoft are safer but grow slower. Small caps can explode — or collapse.
EPS — Earnings Per Share +
How much profit the company made for each share of stock. Higher EPS = more profitable per share. When a company "beats earnings" it means EPS was higher than analysts expected.
Growing EPS each quarter — healthyFlat EPS — no growthNegative or falling EPS — losing money
EPS growth matters more than the raw number. A company growing EPS 20% year over year is very attractive.
Price to Book (P/B) +
Compares a stock's price to the company's "book value" — what it would be worth if it sold everything it owns and paid all debts. A P/B under 1 means you're buying $1 of assets for less than $1.
Under 1 — trading below assets1–3 — reasonableOver 3 — priced for future growth
Very useful for banks and insurance companies. Less useful for tech companies where value is in ideas, not physical assets.
Profitability
Profit Margin +
What percentage of revenue the company actually keeps as profit after paying all expenses. If a company makes $100M in sales and keeps $20M, its profit margin is 20%.
Over 20% — excellent10–20% — healthyUnder 10% — thin or unprofitable
Software companies often have 30–40% margins. Grocery stores might have 2–3%. Always compare within the same industry.
ROE — Return on Equity +
How much profit the company generates from shareholders' money. If investors put in $100 and the company earns $15, ROE is 15%. It measures how efficiently management uses your money.
Over 15% — excellent management8–15% — solidUnder 8% — inefficient use of capital
Warren Buffett looks for companies with consistently high ROE. It's a sign of a strong competitive advantage.
Revenue Growth +
How much the company's total sales increased compared to last year. It's the most basic sign that a company is expanding and people are buying more of what it sells.
Over 15% — fast growing5–15% — steady growthNegative — shrinking business
High growth is exciting but check if it's profitable growth. A company growing revenue but losing more money each year is a red flag.
Risk & Volatility
Beta +
Measures how much a stock moves compared to the overall market. Beta of 1.5 means if the market drops 10%, this stock typically drops 15%. Beta of 0.5 means it would only drop 5%.
Under 1 — calmer than market1.0–1.5 — similar to marketOver 1.5 — wilder than market
If you're nervous about market crashes, stick to low-beta stocks. If you want bigger swings for bigger gains, high beta is for you.
Debt to Equity +
How much the company borrowed compared to what shareholders own. A ratio of 2 means for every $1 of equity, the company owes $2 in debt. High debt = higher risk, especially when interest rates rise.
Under 0.5 — low debt, safe0.5–1.5 — manageableOver 2 — heavy debt load
Some industries like utilities and real estate naturally carry more debt — that's normal. For tech or retail, high debt is a warning sign.
Current Ratio +
Can the company pay its short-term bills? Divides current assets (cash, inventory) by current liabilities (bills due soon). A ratio above 1 means yes, it can cover what it owes in the next year.
Over 2 — very healthy1–2 — adequateUnder 1 — may struggle to pay bills
Think of it like a personal budget. If you have $2,000 in the bank and owe $1,000 this month, your current ratio is 2 — you're fine.
Technical Analysis
RSI — Relative Strength Index +
A 0–100 number that shows if a stock has been bought or sold too aggressively. Think of it as a "temperature gauge" for a stock's momentum. It doesn't predict direction — it spots extremes.
Under 30 — oversold, possible bounce30–70 — neutral zoneOver 70 — overbought, possible pullback
RSI works best combined with other signals. A stock can stay overbought for months if the trend is strong enough.
Moving Average (MA) +
The average price of a stock over the last 50 (or 200) days. It smooths out daily noise and shows the overall trend. Traders watch whether the price is above or below this line to decide direction.
Price above MA — uptrend, bullishPrice below MA — downtrend, bearish
When the 50-day average crosses above the 200-day average, it's called a "Golden Cross" — historically a bullish signal.
52-Week High / Low +
The highest and lowest price the stock has traded at in the past year. If a stock is near its 52-week high, it has strong momentum. Near its low, it might be a bargain — or in trouble.
Near 52-week high — strong momentumMiddle of range — neutralNear 52-week low — weakness or opportunity
Many investors avoid buying near 52-week lows until they understand why the stock fell. Find the reason before assuming it's a bargain.
Price Target +
A price level you want to watch — either as a point to buy (below current price) or a point where you'd consider selling (above current price). Setting an alert at your target tells you when the stock reaches it so you can take a closer look.
Near 52-week low — potential entry if fundamentals are solidMiddle of range — neutral zoneNear or above 52-week high — extended, proceed carefully
A simple starting point: look at the 52-week range. A stock at $150 with a range of $100–$200 is in the middle of its range. Setting an alert at $130 tells you if it pulls back to a historically cheaper level. Use the Analyze tab to check the score before acting.
Investing Basics
Large Cap / Mid Cap / Small Cap +
A way to classify companies by their total market value. Large caps ($10B+) are household names like Apple or Walmart — stable and well-established. Mid caps ($2B–$10B) are growing companies with more potential but more risk. Small caps (under $2B) are younger or niche companies — highest risk, highest reward potential.
Large cap $10B+ — stable, lower riskMid cap $2B–$10B — balanced growthSmall cap under $2B — high risk, high reward
Micro caps (under $300M) are even smaller and far more volatile — they can double or lose 80% in a year. Stick to large and mid caps when starting out.
TAM — Total Addressable Market +
The total revenue opportunity available to a company if it captured 100% of its target market. A company selling project management software might have a TAM of $20B — meaning the entire global market for that type of software is worth $20B/year. A large TAM doesn't guarantee success, but a tiny TAM limits how big a company can ever get.
Large TAM + low market share = huge room to growMedium TAM + strong position = steady businessSmall TAM + mature market = limited upside
Investors love companies with massive TAMs and less than 10% market share — there's still enormous room to grow. But always check if the company is actually winning market share, not just operating in a big space.
Dividend +
A cash payment companies make to shareholders, usually every quarter. If you own 100 shares and the dividend is $0.50/share, you get $50. It's like getting paid just for holding the stock.
Yield 2–4% — solid incomeYield 1–2% — modestYield over 6% — may be unsustainable
A very high dividend yield can be a trap — it might mean the stock price dropped sharply, or the company can't sustain the payment.
Bull & Bear Market +
A bull market is when prices are rising and investor confidence is high (think bull charging forward). A bear market is when prices fall 20%+ from recent highs and fear dominates (bear swiping down).
Bull market — rising prices, optimismBear market — falling 20%+, pessimism
Bear markets are scary but historically temporary. The S&P 500 has recovered from every single bear market in history.
Diversification +
Spreading your money across different stocks, industries, or asset types so one bad investment doesn't ruin your whole portfolio. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
5–15 stocks across sectors — well diversified3–5 stocks — some risk concentration1–2 stocks — very concentrated risk
Owning 10 tech stocks is NOT diversification — they all tend to move together. True diversification means different industries.
Portfolio +
Your complete collection of investments — all the stocks, ETFs, or other assets you own. Tracking your portfolio helps you see your total gains/losses and whether you're properly diversified.
Balanced across sectors — lower risk2–3 sector focus — moderate riskOne sector only — concentrated risk
Review your portfolio allocation regularly. If one stock grows a lot, it might become too big a percentage of your total — consider rebalancing.
ETF — Exchange Traded Fund +
A basket of stocks you can buy as a single investment. Buying SPY (S&P 500 ETF) is like buying a tiny piece of the 500 biggest US companies at once. Instant diversification, low cost.
Index ETFs — broadest diversificationSector ETFs — focused on one industryLeveraged ETFs — very risky, for experts only
Warren Buffett recommends most people just buy a low-cost S&P 500 ETF instead of picking individual stocks.
Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA) +
Investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals — like $100 every month — regardless of the stock price. When prices are low you buy more shares; when they're high you buy fewer. Over time it smooths out the impact of volatility.
Consistent schedule — removes emotion from decisionsOccasional investing — better than nothingTiming the market — rarely works long term
Studies consistently show that DCA beats trying to "time" the market for most investors. Set it and forget it.
Compounding +
Earning returns on your returns. If you invest $1,000 and it grows 10%, you have $1,100. Next year 10% on $1,100 = $110 — not just $100. Over decades this snowball effect is the most powerful force in investing.
10+ years — compounding really kicks in5–10 years — noticeable growthUnder 5 years — limited compounding effect
$10,000 invested at 8% annual return becomes ~$46,600 in 20 years and ~$100,600 in 30 years. Time in the market is everything.
Rebalancing +
Periodically adjusting your portfolio back to your intended allocation. If tech stocks surge and now represent 60% of your portfolio instead of 30%, rebalancing means trimming tech and adding to other sectors to restore balance.
Rebalance annually or when off target by 10%+Every 2–3 years — acceptableNever rebalancing — can lead to unintended high concentration
Rebalancing forces you to "sell high and buy low" automatically — you trim what grew a lot and add to what's cheaper.
Earnings Report / Earnings Season +
Every quarter (3 months), public companies report their financial results — revenue, profit, EPS — compared to what analysts expected. Earnings season is when most companies report at the same time (January, April, July, October). Stock prices often move sharply after earnings.
Beat expectations — stock often jumpsMet expectations — usually muted reactionMissed expectations — stock often drops
Even a great earnings beat can cause a drop if "guidance" (future outlook) disappoints. Always read both the results AND the company's forward outlook.
Stock Split +
When a company divides its existing shares into more shares at a lower price. A 2-for-1 split means if you owned 10 shares at $200 each, you now own 20 shares at $100 each. The total value is the same — it's just more affordable per share.
Makes shares more accessible to smaller investorsNo change in company value or fundamentalsReverse split (1-for-10) can signal financial trouble
Apple has split its stock 5 times. Without splits, one share would cost thousands of dollars today. Splits are usually a sign of a healthy, growing stock.
Inflation & Real Returns +
Inflation is the gradual rise in prices over time — $100 today buys less than $100 ten years ago. "Real return" is your investment gain minus inflation. If your portfolio grew 6% but inflation was 3%, your real return is only 3%.
Real return above 4% — beating inflation comfortablyReal return 1–4% — modest gainsReal return negative — losing purchasing power
Keeping cash in a savings account at 1% while inflation runs at 3% means you're losing purchasing power every year. Investing is partly about not falling behind inflation.
Valuation (Advanced)
Free Cash Flow (FCF) +
The actual cash a company generates after paying for its operations and capital expenditures (buildings, equipment). Unlike profit, FCF is hard to fake — it's real money that can be used for dividends, buybacks, debt paydown, or growth.
Positive and growing FCF — financially strongPositive but flat FCF — stable, not growingNegative FCF — burning cash, needs funding
A company can show accounting profit but negative free cash flow. Always check FCF alongside net income — it tells the real story.
PEG Ratio +
Price/Earnings-to-Growth ratio. Takes the P/E ratio and divides it by the earnings growth rate. A company with a P/E of 30 growing at 30% per year has a PEG of 1 — considered fair value. It fixes the P/E's blind spot: it accounts for how fast the company is growing.
Under 1 — potentially undervalued for its growth1–2 — fairly valuedOver 2 — expensive even accounting for growth
Peter Lynch popularized PEG. He called anything under 1 a potential bargain. It's most useful for fast-growing companies where P/E alone looks expensive.
EBITDA +
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A measure of a company's core operating profitability that strips out financing decisions and accounting rules. Often used to compare companies across industries or countries.
High and growing EBITDA — strong operationsStable EBITDA — consistent but not growingDeclining EBITDA — core business weakening
EBITDA is useful but can be misleading — critics call it "earnings before bad stuff." A company with great EBITDA but terrible free cash flow may still be in trouble.
Enterprise Value (EV) +
The "true" total cost to buy a company — market cap plus debt minus cash. If a company has a $10B market cap, $3B in debt, and $1B in cash, its EV is $12B. It's what an acquirer would actually pay to take over the whole business.
Low EV/EBITDA under 10 — potentially cheapEV/EBITDA 10–20 — fairly valuedEV/EBITDA over 20 — expensive or high-growth expectations
EV/EBITDA is often more useful than P/E for comparing companies with different debt levels. Two companies with the same P/E can look very different when you include their debt.
Technical Analysis (Advanced)
Volume +
The number of shares traded during a period. High volume on a big price move confirms the move is real and supported by many investors. Low volume on a move suggests it might be weak and reverse.
High volume + price rise — strong, confirmed moveAverage volume — normal dayLow volume + big move — potentially unreliable signal
If a stock jumps 5% on very low volume, be skeptical. If it jumps 5% on 3× normal volume, more investors are genuinely participating in the move.
Support & Resistance +
Support is a price level where a stock has repeatedly stopped falling — buyers step in. Resistance is a level where it has repeatedly stopped rising — sellers step in. These levels act like a floor and ceiling on price.
Price bouncing off support — bullish signPrice consolidating between levels — neutralPrice breaking through support — bearish signal
When a stock breaks through a resistance level with high volume, that old resistance often becomes the new support. Traders call this a "breakout."
MACD +
Moving Average Convergence Divergence. A momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of a stock's price. When the MACD line crosses above the signal line, it's considered a bullish signal; crossing below is bearish.
MACD crosses above signal line — bullish momentumMACD near zero — no strong trendMACD crosses below signal line — bearish momentum
MACD is a lagging indicator — it confirms trends rather than predicting them. Best used alongside RSI and volume for a more complete picture.
Short Interest +
The percentage of a company's shares that investors have borrowed and sold, betting the price will fall. High short interest means many people expect the stock to drop. If they're wrong and the stock rises, shorts are forced to buy back shares — causing a "short squeeze."
Under 5% — low short interest, normal5–15% — elevated bearish sentimentOver 20% — heavily shorted, squeeze risk both ways
GameStop in 2021 is the famous short squeeze example — short interest was over 100%, retail investors piled in, and shorts lost billions. High short interest is a double-edged sword.
Risk Management
Interest Coverage Ratio +
How many times a company can pay its interest expenses with its operating profit. A ratio of 5 means the company earns 5× what it owes in interest. It shows whether debt is manageable or dangerous.
Over 5× — very safe, easily covers debt payments3–5× — adequate but watch closelyUnder 2× — dangerously close to not covering interest
In a recession, revenues fall. A company with an interest coverage of 1.5× in good times might fall below 1× when business slows — that means it can't pay its debt.
Gross Margin vs. Net Margin +
Gross margin is revenue minus cost of goods sold — what's left before operating expenses like salaries and rent. Net margin is what's left after everything, including taxes and interest. The gap between them reveals how lean or bloated a company's operations are.
High gross, high net — efficient and profitableHigh gross, low net — high operating costs eating profitsLow gross margin — tough business model, hard to be profitable
A software company might have 80% gross margins but only 15% net margins due to heavy R&D and sales costs. That's fine if those costs are building a moat. A retailer with 20% gross margins has very little room for error.
Market Sentiment +
The overall attitude of investors toward the market or a specific stock — whether they're feeling greedy (bullish) or fearful (bearish). Sentiment can drive prices far above or below what fundamentals justify, especially in the short term.
Fearful market — historically good long-term entry pointsNeutral sentiment — prices roughly reflect fundamentalsExtreme greed — markets often overvalued, risk of correction
Warren Buffett's famous rule: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." The CNN Fear & Greed Index is a popular sentiment tracker.
Share Buyback +
When a company uses its own cash to repurchase its shares from the open market. This reduces the total number of shares outstanding, which increases earnings per share (EPS) for remaining shareholders — even if total profit didn't change.
Buybacks when stock is undervalued — great use of cashBuybacks at fair value — neutral to slightly positiveBuybacks when overvalued — destroys shareholder value
Apple has spent over $500 billion buying back its own stock. This is one reason EPS has grown faster than revenue — fewer shares means more earnings per share.
Sectors & Industries
Sector +
A broad grouping of companies with similar business activities. The stock market is typically divided into 11 sectors (e.g., Technology, Healthcare, Energy). Sectors help investors compare companies on a level playing field and spread risk across different parts of the economy.
Diversified across sectors — lower overall riskConcentrated in 2-3 sectors — moderate riskAll in one sector — high concentration risk
The S&P 500 is split into 11 GICS sectors. Technology is the largest (~30%), while Utilities and Real Estate are among the smallest.
Cyclical vs Defensive Stocks +
Cyclical stocks rise and fall with the economy (e.g., autos, travel, luxury goods) — they do well in booms and suffer in recessions. Defensive stocks hold steady regardless of the economic cycle (e.g., utilities, food, healthcare) because people always need those products.
Defensive stocks — stable in downturnsMixed portfolio — balanced exposureAll cyclicals — vulnerable in recessions
During the 2020 COVID crash, Zoom (cyclical-ish tech) surged while airlines collapsed. Consumer staples like Procter & Gamble barely moved — that's defensive strength.
Sector Rotation +
The strategy of moving money between sectors as the economic cycle changes. For example, investors might shift into Energy and Materials during expansion, then rotate into Utilities and Healthcare as a recession approaches. Each phase of the economic cycle tends to favor different sectors.
In the right sector at the right cycle phaseNeutral sector mix — market-like returnsIn the wrong sector for the current cycle
A classic rotation: as interest rates rise, money flows out of high-growth Tech (hurt by higher discount rates) and into Financials (banks earn more on loans).
Industry vs Sector +
A sector is the broad category; an industry is the specific niche within it. For example, Healthcare is a sector. Within it, you have industries like Pharmaceuticals, Biotech, and Medical Devices. Companies in the same industry compete directly, while sector peers may have very different business models.
Comparing within the same industry — apples to applesComparing within a sector — useful but less preciseComparing across sectors — very different dynamics
Apple and Visa are both in different industries (Consumer Electronics vs Financial Technology) but Wall Street often groups them together as "large-cap growth." Always check the specific industry when comparing P/E ratios.
Industry P/E (Relative Valuation) +
Every industry has its own "normal" P/E range based on growth expectations and risk. A P/E of 15x might be expensive for a utility but cheap for a software company. Comparing a stock's P/E to its industry average tells you whether it's trading at a premium or discount relative to peers.
P/E below industry average — potentially undervalued vs peersP/E near industry average — fairly valuedP/E far above industry average — needs strong justification
Bank stocks often trade at 10-12x earnings. Tech stocks at 25-40x. Comparing a bank's P/E to a tech stock's P/E tells you nothing — always compare within the same industry.
Market Concentration & Moat +
Market concentration refers to how dominated an industry is by a few players. A "moat" (coined by Warren Buffett) is a company's durable competitive advantage — what makes it hard for competitors to take market share. Moats can come from brand, patents, network effects, cost advantages, or switching costs.
Wide moat — dominant, hard to displaceNarrow moat — some competitive edgeNo moat — easily competed away
Google controls ~90% of search. That's a massive moat. A local restaurant chain has no moat — anyone can copy the menu. Moat strength directly affects how sustainable a company's margins are.
Macro Sensitivity +
How sensitive a sector or stock is to macroeconomic factors like interest rates, inflation, GDP growth, or commodity prices. Energy stocks move with oil prices. Real estate stocks are sensitive to interest rates. Tech growth stocks are hurt by rising rates. Understanding macro sensitivity helps you anticipate how external events might impact your holdings.
Low macro sensitivity — predictable in most environmentsModerate sensitivity — some economic exposureHigh sensitivity — big moves on macro news
When the Fed raised rates aggressively in 2022, the NASDAQ dropped ~33% while energy stocks surged 50%+. Macro matters — a lot.
